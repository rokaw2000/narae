The Red-Crowned Crane
A symbol of longevity,
grace, and endurance
Grus japonensis — the red-crowned crane is one of the rarest and most revered birds on Earth. Across Korea, Japan, and China, it has been honored for centuries as a messenger of heaven, a companion of the wise, and a symbol of the quiet strength that endures. Fewer than 3,000 remain in the wild. It is the spirit of naræ.
Korea 한국
두루미 Durumi
Symbol of longevity, nobility, and faithful love. The crane was said to carry departed souls to heaven and appears on royal robes and celadon pottery for over 1,000 years. On the 500-won coin today.
Japan 日本
鶴 Tsuru
Lives 1,000 years in legend. Folding 1,000 origami cranes — senbazuru — grants a wish. Symbol of fidelity: cranes mate for life. Given as a wedding blessing and a prayer for healing.
China 中国
仙鶴 Xiānhè
The "immortal crane" carries Taoist sages to heaven. Represents longevity, wisdom, and purity. Painted alongside pine trees and clouds in classical scroll art for over two millennia.
naræ
나래 Wings
The quiet strength to rise again — fold by fold, thread by thread. The crane in the naræ mark carries the same spirit: not loud, but steady. Not instant, but enduring.
Conservation facts
01
Fewer than 3,000 red-crowned cranes remain in the wild — one of the rarest crane species on Earth, listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List and declining.
02
They stand nearly 5 feet tall with a wingspan up to 8 feet — the second tallest crane in the world. The red crown is not feathers but bare red skin that brightens during courtship.
03
The DMZ between North and South Korea — one of the world's most restricted zones — has paradoxically become a critical habitat. About 30% of the entire wild population winters in the Cheorwon Basin each year.
04
They mate for life, sometimes living over 25 years. Their courtship dance — leaping, bowing, spreading wings — is one of the most beautiful rituals in the natural world.
05
In Korean culture, seeing a crane is still considered a sign of long life and grace. The poet Hwang Sunwon's 1953 story "Cranes" uses the bird as a symbol of humanity and reconciliation across the division of war.

Hiroshige's woodblock cranes are always in motion — ascending, grouped, purposeful. They express the Japanese ideal of graceful, collective endurance, a quality naræ honors in every woman.

Goryeo celadon — the pinnacle of Korean ceramic art — frequently features inlaid cranes and clouds. The National Museum of Korea holds works considered among the most exquisite pottery ever made.

Auspicious Cranes (瑞鶴圖)
Emperor Huizong · China · Song Dynasty, c. 1112
Twenty cranes circle above the imperial palace gate — believed to signal divine blessing. One of the most celebrated works in all of Chinese art history, painted by an emperor himself.
The origami crane is one of the most recognized paper-folding forms in the world. In Japanese tradition, folding 1,000 cranes — senbazuru — is believed to grant a wish or bring healing. It takes patience, repetition, and care: the same qualities that shape recovery from illness.
Begin with a square sheet of paper. The crane emerges from a single sheet — no cuts, no glue. Only folds. Like healing itself: shaped gradually, with intention.
1
The square base
Fold diagonally both ways, then horizontally both ways. Collapse into a small square with the open end facing you. This is the preliminary base — the foundation of nearly all origami.
2
The petal fold
Fold both right and left edges to the center crease, then unfold. Lift the top layer, opening and flattening into a diamond shape. Repeat on the other side. This is the bird base.
3
The narrow wings
Fold both lower flaps upward along the center crease, pointing upward. These two points will become the neck and the tail.
4
The inside reverse fold
On one of the upward points, make an inside reverse fold to create the head. A small fold at the very tip forms the beak — precise and intentional.
5
Open the wings
Hold the neck and tail and gently pull apart. The body opens and inflates. The crane takes form — patient, deliberate, complete. Every fold was necessary.
