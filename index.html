The meaning of naræ+

Inspired by the Korean word for wing, naræ+ reflects lift, tenderness, and motion. The red-crowned crane symbolizes grace, longevity, hope, and devoted connection. The plus mark represents something added: support, possibility, and one more hand extended toward another.

About

A community shaped by care, beauty, and meaningful presence.

naræ International Foundation is not built around urgency or noise. It is being built with intention. The vision is to create a refined, welcoming community where people can encounter encouragement, participate with sincerity, and contribute in ways that feel human, grounded, and real.

Illness and hardship can leave people isolated in fear, exhaustion, and darkness. naræ+ seeks to hold space for another direction: one marked by dignity, hope, cultural care, creativity, and connection.

naræ+ is envisioned as more than a single program. It can include writing circles, music sharing, cultural exchange, volunteer-led gatherings, gentle conversation spaces, language support, care-centered projects, and other opportunities that help people remain connected over time.

1000 Crane Project

A living ritual of remembrance, hope, and community care.

The 1000 Crane Project is envisioned as an evolving naræ+ ritual. Over time, it will symbolize 1000 women helped, seen, encouraged, or held in community through the foundation’s work.

How it can grow

Each crane is more than a symbol. It can represent one life touched with dignity and care. As the community grows, naræ Fellows and future participants may each create a crane as part of a shared act of reflection, encouragement, and belonging.

One crane, one woman remembered Each crane can symbolize a woman supported, honored, or uplifted through the naræ+ community.
A ritual created by naræ Fellows Fellows may fold and dedicate a crane as a visible gesture of care, witness, and connection.
A growing installation of hope Over time, the cranes can become a collective visual story of healing, resilience, and lives touched.
Why it matters

From symbol to embodied community practice

This project helps translate the mission into something people can feel and participate in. It carries beauty, memory, and movement. It also gives the community a meaningful way to mark growth without reducing women to numbers alone.

The goal is not performance. It is reverence. The cranes can quietly testify that care reached someone, that support mattered, and that no one should have to disappear into suffering unseen.
